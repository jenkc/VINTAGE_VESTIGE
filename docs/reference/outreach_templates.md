## Personalization Notes

When customizing these templates:

1. **Research the recipient first.** If the museum just launched a new digital
   initiative, reference it. If the dataset owner published a paper, cite it.
   "I saw your recent work on [X] and thought..."

2. **Include one specific example** from their collection if possible. "I noticed
   your collection includes [specific item]. Our system would connect it to..."

3. **Match their language.** Museums say "holdings" and "objects." Academics say
   "dataset" and "annotations." Vintage dealers say "pieces" and "items."

4. **Lead with what they get, not what you need.** The enriched metadata and
   cross-collection connections are genuinely valuable to them. Make that the
   headline.

5. **Keep it short.** Museum digital teams and researchers get a lot of email.
   Template C is probably the right length for a first touch. Follow up with
   Template A/B if they respond.

## Target List

### Museums with digital costume collections + open access APIs:
- Victoria & Albert Museum (London) — largest fashion collection in the world
- LACMA (Los Angeles County Museum of Art) — strong costume collection
- Philadelphia Museum of Art — good American fashion holdings
- Chicago History Museum — American fashion + social history
- Museum at FIT — the fashion school museum, digitized collection
- Kyoto Costume Institute — Japanese + Western fashion, exceptional quality

### Academic datasets:
- iDesigner (Kaggle) — 50K runway images, 50 designers, MIT license
- DeepFashion / DeepFashion2 — large-scale fashion recognition dataset
- ModaNet — street fashion with segmentation masks
- Fashion-MNIST — simple but good for benchmarking

### Cultural heritage aggregators:
- Europeana — 50M+ items from European institutions, single API
- DPLA (Digital Public Library of America) — aggregates US collections
- Wikimedia Commons — fashion category has thousands of historical images
